International Policy and Recycling


Global agreements are key to defeating climate change; international climate activism has gained lots of traction. In this section of the wesbite we discuss some of the movements and changes made across the world that can really make a difference, especially in an era that we are getting more and more access to global news and interest in the international political sphere. Some of the few topics covered in this page include:

  • Multilteral Climate Agreements:

    Green new deal, Paris Accord, and The Montreal Protocol

  • Successful International Recycling Models

    • Kamikatsu, Japan: Waste Segregation
    • Swiss Recycling: Polluter Pays principle
    • Taipei, Taiwan: Extended Producer Responsibility

  • Foreign Policy Enforcement:

    UN Compliance fines and accountability, A closed loop economic system

Economic Change




Without change in the industrial/commercial/military industry, individual/family changes are almost meaningless. Pressure is being put on businesses + corporations to think aboust material sustainability and recyclability. Industrial waste makes up a staggering amount of the percentage of non-recycled or non-renewable waste in the world. Making businessess accountable for their material waste means better designed packaging and an imporved sorting system. Industrial capacity to practice reduction, reuse, and recycling, but larger scale change might be needed




A closed loop economic model is one approach to this that is currently being implemented in many European countries; its aim is similar to the zero-waste in that the product lifecycle is designed to be ongoing past an initial period of reuse, and repairability is accesssible and prefferable over replacement. Because of the difficulty of swiching over from a "single-use" economy, government incentives combined with consumer pressure are necessary to begin a shift in this direction for the U.S.
